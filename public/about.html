A Downtown Tower · 1994

The tide went out in 1889.
Something stayed.

Seasons Gone Bye is a serialized fiction podcast: grunge-era Seattle above, ancient faerie courts on the floors above that, in the tallest building west of the Mississippi.

The premise

The lobby smells of coffee and something older than rain. On the first floor of an old downtown tower there is a café called Seasons Gone Bye. It keeps two sets of hours: the ones painted on the glass, and the ones the regulars know. You are listening during the second kind.

Above the café, four ancient courts have kept the boundary between the seen and the unseen since before the city had a name. They collect what is owed. They hold what is promised. They keep the door.

Now the courts are failing. Payments come late. The coffee comes out cold. A visitor asks the elevator for a floor that is not on the panel — and the elevator knows the way.

Four courts. One tower. Last call is at moonset.

FROM THE COLD OPEN · EP.01

Who keeps what

The Spring Court

Keeps the seeds

Everything that insists on growing back — grudges, weeds, second chances. Spring counts what returns, and it always returns.

The Summer Court

Keeps the deeds

Owns land the maps stopped showing. They flattened Denny Hill a century ago; Summer still holds the deed to the dirt they hauled into the bay.

The Autumn Court

Keeps the debts

Every bargain, itemized; every favor, filed. Interest accrues in seasons, and Autumn never forgives a balance. It only ever forecloses.

The Winter Court

Keeps the door

The last court still fully staffed. Winter guards the threshold between the seen and the unseen — and lately, more of the unseen has been coming through than going back.

How it sounds

Wry noir over an earnest, mythic floor — a narrator who has seen too much, in a city that remembers more. The supernatural is stated, never exclaimed. There is rain, tape hiss, and one warm light at the end of a wet street.

It is made for people raised on Grimm, Lost Girl, The X-Files, and American Gods; for listeners of Old Gods of Appalachia; and for anyone who remembers what 1994 sounded like — equally.
